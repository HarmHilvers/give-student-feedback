# Feedback Guidelines

## Why Feedback Matters

Feedback strongly influences learning outcomes when it is informative, reaches the student clearly, and leads to action. Its effect depends on what the student does with it after receiving it.

## Feed Up, Feed Back, Feed Forward

Use the three-question model:

- Feed up: What is the student working toward?
- Feed back: How is the student doing right now?
- Feed forward: What can the student do next to reach the goal?

Operationalize these as:

- learning goals, assignment expectations, or rubric criteria
- evaluation of the current work or performance
- improvement directions that help the student move forward

## Constructive Feedback Principles

- Be specific about the exact part of the work the feedback addresses.
- Refer to a concrete situation, passage, or step in the process where possible.
- Leave room for student response or reflection.
- Focus on the work, not on the student as a person.
- Describe what you observe before interpreting causes.
- Include both strengths and improvement points.
- Point the student in a direction instead of solving the task for them.

## Written Feedback Guidance

Written feedback lets students revisit comments in their own time, but it also creates room for misinterpretation because the teacher is absent at the moment of reading. When useful, invite a response or suggest a follow-up discussion.

Be careful with rewriting student text to "fix" it. Use rewriting sparingly to illustrate a point, not to remove the student's opportunity to learn through revision.
